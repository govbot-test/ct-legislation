OLR Bill Analysis
sSB 410
AN ACT CONCERNING FIREFIGHTER CANCER.
SUMMARY
This bill extends certain benefits to survivors of firefighters who (1)
die of certain cancers and (2) would be eligible for benefits from the
firefighters cancer relief account (see BACKGROUND). Specifically, it:
1. extends Fallen Hero Fund benefits to these firefighters’ surviving
families or beneficiaries,
2. allows certain surviving family members who were covered
under the firefighter’s health care benefit plan at the time of their
death to apply for or keep the coverage for a specified period of
time, and
3. requires these benefits to be funded by the firefighters cancer
relief account.
The bill also requires, by October 1, 2026, the State Fire Marshal to
establish a one-year pilot program to reimburse firefighters in two
municipalities who get screenings for cancers common to firefighters.
Lastly, the bill makes technical and conforming changes.
EFFECTIVE DATE: July 1, 2026
FALLEN HERO FUND BENEFITS
By law, the Fallen Hero Fund pays $100,000, within available
appropriations, to the surviving families of first responders (emergency
medical technicians, paid firefighters, paramedics, and police officers)
killed in the line of duty or who sustained injuries that were the direct
or proximate cause of the first responder’s death.

The bill extends this same benefit to the surviving families of
firefighters who:
1. were diagnosed with, and died from, any cancer affecting the
skin or brain or the skeletal, digestive, endocrine, respiratory,
lymphatic, reproductive, urinary, or hematological systems, and
2. would be entitled to benefits from the firefighters cancer relief
account on or after October 1, 2023.
As under existing law for first responders killed in the line of duty,
the “surviving family” is a surviving spouse, surviving child (whether
dependent or not), or surviving parent of the firefighter, or the most
recently listed surviving beneficiary on file with the employer.
Under the bill, payments to these surviving families must be made
from the firefighters cancer relief account, rather than the Fallen Hero
Fund as existing law requires for payments to surviving families of
other first responders.
By law, the comptroller must annually report to the Public Safety and
Security Committee on the expenditures made from the Fallen Hero
Fund for the prior year, the fund’s current balance, and information on
additional amounts needed for the fund. The bill expands this reporting
requirement to also include the same information for the firefighters
cancer relief account.
PARTNERSHIP PLAN COVERAGE
By law, non-state public employers that provided coverage under a
“partnership plan” (health benefit plans the comptroller offers to these
and other employers) to a first responder who is killed in the line of duty
must continue to provide the coverage to the survivors who were
covered under the plan at the time of the first responder’s death. The bill
requires these employers to provide this same coverage to survivors of
firefighters who died of cancer, as described above. As under existing
law, the coverage must continue for one year after the death and may
be renewed annually for up to five years. The non-state public employer

As under existing law for first responders, under the bill, a nonstate

as existing law requires for survivors of other first responders.
CANCER SCREENING PILOT PROGRAM
By October 1, 2026, the State Fire Marshal must establish a one-year
pilot program to reimburse paid firefighters in two municipalities who
get cancer screenings for cancers that are common to firefighters.
“Firefighter” refers to any (1) local or deputy fire marshal, fire
investigator, fire inspector, and other classes of inspectors and
investigators for whom the State Fire Marshal and the Codes and
Standards Committee have jointly adopted minimum qualification
standards, and (2) uniformed member of a paid municipal, state, or
volunteer fire department.
The State Fire Marshal must consult with a nonprofit with expertise
in the field of cancer screening to determine which screenings qualify
for the pilot program’s reimbursement. The pilot program must be
operated in one municipality served by a volunteer fire department and

BACKGROUND
Firefighters Cancer Relief Account
By law, the firefighters cancer relief account generally provides, for
eligible paid firefighters diagnosed with certain cancers and die or
become disabled because of it, (1) workers compensation benefits, (2)
retirement and survivor benefits, and (3) certain disability benefits
available from the Connecticut State Firefighters Association. To be
eligible, the firefighter must be diagnosed with certain cancers, and (1)
have shown no sign of cancer at a medical evaluation after entering into
service, (2) not have smoked cigarettes for 15-years prior to the cancer
diagnosis, (3) been employed for at least five years in certain firefighting
positions, and (4) have submitted to annual medical screenings as
recommended by their medical provider.
Beginning January 1, 2027, the account will be funded by a five cent
per month fee, per service line, on each subscriber to a telephone service
provider (including cell phone and Internet phone service providers,
but not for certain prepaid cell phone services).
