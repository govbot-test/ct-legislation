Explanation
The bill results in a potential cost to the Department of Mental Health
and Addiction Services (DMHAS) due to eliminating the requirement
that DMHAS terminate the psychedelic-assisted therapy (PAT) pilot
program when the federal Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)
approves MDMA and psilocybin for medical use. To the extent the pilot
would have otherwise ended due to DEA approval, DMHAS may now
continue to operate the pilot under the bill.
The bill also expands eligibility for the pilot, which may impact
participant mix but does not require DMHAS to support additional
clients.
The Out Years
The annualized ongoing fiscal impact identified above would
continue into the future subject to federal DEA approvals.

OLR Bill Analysis
SB 191
AN ACT CONCERNING THE PSYCHEDELIC-ASSISTED THERAPY
PILOT PROGRAM.
SUMMARY
This bill expands eligibility for the Department of Mental Health and
Addiction Services (DMHAS) psychedelic-assisted therapy (PAT) pilot
program by allowing Connecticut residents to participate who (1) are at
least age 18 and (2) meet clinical eligibility criteria established by the
institutional review board of the medical school who administers it
(currently Yale University). Currently, the pilot program is limited to
Connecticut veterans, retired first responders, and direct health care
workers. As under current law, participants must receive MDMA- (i.e.
“Molly” or “ecstasy”) or psilocybin-assisted therapy as part of a
research program approved by the federal Food and Drug
Administration (FDA).
The bill also eliminates the current requirement that DMHAS end the
pilot program if the federal Drug Enforcement Agency (DEA) approves
MDMA and psilocybin for medical use (the DEA has not yet done so).
MDMA is a synthetic psychoactive drug, and psilocybin occurs
naturally in some mushrooms. Both act as serotonin receptor agonists,
and MDMA also acts as a reuptake inhibitor of serotonin and dopamine.
